# Rewriting

Morning came softly, almost shy. Light crept in through the curtains, pale and golden, spilling across the floorboards. The air was still cool, carrying the smell of rain and pine. Somewhere outside, I could hear a kettle whistling and the sound of someone sweeping the verandah — that slow, steady rhythm that always feels like the start of a new day in the hills.

I stepped out, mug in hand, and the world felt fresh again. Mist floated lazily between the trees, the sky still half-asleep. The road below curved through the forest like a secret no one had bothered to hide. For a moment, I just stood there — breathing, watching, letting the silence sink in.

We set out for the hike. The trail wasn’t easy — steep in places, narrow in others — but every step was worth it. The trees stood tall around us like old guardians, their branches swaying as if whispering stories from another time. The wind carried the smell of wet leaves, and somewhere in the distance, birds sang songs we didn’t recognise but somehow felt we understood.

With every step deeper into the forest, it felt like we were walking away from the world — and closer to something we couldn’t name.

And then, we saw it.

The waterfall.

It looked unreal. Water fell in long silver ribbons, catching the sunlight so it looked like the air itself was made of stars. The pool below shimmered like it held a thousand old secrets. For a moment, no one said a word. We just stood there, eyes wide, hearts quiet.

Then someone — maybe me, maybe not — ran forward, and suddenly we were all in. Laughing. Shouting. Splashing around like kids who had forgotten what worry even meant. Cold water on our faces, sun on our backs, joy in its purest form. Our clothes clung to us, our shoes filled with water, and for once, nobody cared.

For that one hour, time forgot us. The world outside the valley didn’t exist.

And somewhere in the quiet rhythm of falling water, I felt something stir inside me. Not loud. Not clear. But there.

Something — or someone — waiting.

By the time we returned to the hotel, the sun had dipped low. Our legs ached, our clothes still damp, but our hearts were warm. The stillness of the mountains had done something to us — softened us, slowed us down, reminded us of who we were beneath all the noise.

That night, we sat again around a small fire outside the hotel, wrapped in blankets, sipping hot chai. We laughed about the day — who slipped first, who screamed loudest — but beneath the laughter, there was this quiet knowing. Something was changing. Something was unfolding, though none of us could name it yet.

When I finally crawled into my cozy tent, the air smelled of smoke and pine. I felt lighter — like I had let go of something I didn’t even know I was holding on to. The moonlight slipped through the flap of the tent, a thin golden thread across the floor. I lay there watching it until sleep slowly pulled me in.

And that night, I dreamed again.

Only this time, it felt real. Vivid. Alive.

I was walking through a place that didn’t belong to this world — not earth, not sky, but something in between. Time didn’t move there. Even silence felt alive, full of meaning. The air around me hummed softly, charged with something sacred. Every step I took felt like it was leading me toward something — or someone — I had always known.

And then… he appeared.

A figure bathed in soft light — not blinding, but warm. Radiating an energy that felt familiar, like peace had taken a human form. I couldn’t move. I couldn’t speak. I could only feel it — every heartbeat, every breath — telling me that this wasn’t just a dream.

Not the Aariv I had yet to meet in this world — but the true Aariv. The one my soul had always known. His eyes carried lifetimes, and his presence felt like a prayer — calm, whole, and familiar in a way that words could never describe.

And then, our eyes met.

Time stopped.

The stars above us went still. The air softened, as if the universe itself had paused to watch.

And without a word, we began to move.

We danced.

Not with our bodies, but with our souls.

It wasn’t a performance — it was remembrance. A rhythm older than time itself. Every step felt known, every turn felt inevitable, like we had done this across ages. It was fluid, timeless — the kind of dance that doesn’t begin or end. Our movements weren’t learned; they were remembered. The way fire remembers heat. The way the ocean remembers the pull of the moon.

And then — it appeared again.

The Divine Union.

The same vision that had visited me before, only this time it was brighter, clearer — almost alive. Half feminine. Half masculine. The ultimate balance of energies.

The feminine side — soft, radiant, nurturing — was me. I could feel it in every breath.

And the masculine side, though still slightly veiled, carried a familiar stillness. The silence. The strength. The depth that could only belong to him — to Aariv.

As we danced, the two halves began to merge — not as two becoming one, but as one remembering itself. The boundary between us dissolved, and in that sacred merging, I understood:

We were never separate. We had simply been waiting for the veil to lift.

When I opened my eyes, I was back in my tent. The mountains were silent outside; dawn had not yet touched the sky. But my heart was wide awake.

This wasn’t a dream.

This was remembrance.

Something ancient had spoken. Something eternal had stirred.

And as I sat up, wrapping the blanket around my shoulders, I felt it deep in my bone.
