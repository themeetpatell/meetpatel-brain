# Sean Ellis 40% PMF Test

> Product-Market Fit is hard to define but easy to measure. Ask your users one question. If 40%+ say they'd be very disappointed without you, you have PMF. Below 40%, you don't — and no amount of acquisition will save you.

## When to use it
- Mid-to-late stage product development (after ~40+ engaged users)
- Pre-fundraise (investors increasingly ask the PMF question this way)
- Before scaling acquisition spend
- Quarterly check on PMF strength — it can erode as you grow

## The framework — one question

Ask all active users:

> *"How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"*
>
> a) Very disappointed
> b) Somewhat disappointed
> c) Not disappointed
> d) N/A — I no longer use it

**The 40% rule:** if 40% or more of users answer "Very disappointed," you have PMF.

Two supporting questions to ask the same survey:
- **"What type of person do you think would most benefit from [product]?"** → reveals the real ICP in their words
- **"What's the main benefit you receive from [product]?"** → reveals the actual job-to-be-done

## How to interpret

- **<25% "very disappointed"** — no PMF. Don't scale. Pivot the product or the segment.
- **25–40%** — early PMF signal. Sharpen ICP, double down on the "very disappointed" cohort's profile.
- **40%+** — PMF achieved. Scale acquisition. Stay close to the "very disappointed" cohort, they're your moat.
- **>50%** — strong PMF. Now the constraint is acquisition channels, not the product.

## How to apply

### Finanshels
Run the survey across all active customers monthly. Segment by:
- Customer tenure (new vs >6 months — new users haven't experienced enough value)
- ICP fit (NRI SMB vs free-zone vs F&B — find which segment hits 40% first)
- Service tier (basic / mid / premium — which tier has the strongest PMF signal?)

Use the "what type of person" answer to refine ICP. If "very disappointed" users all describe the same buyer, that's your real wedge.

### Biggdate (post-launch)
Run at 30, 60, 90 days post-launch. The "very disappointed" cohort defines who Biggdate is *really* for — likely more specific than the marketing ICP.

### StartupOS (post-launch)
Run as soon as 40+ active users are using weekly. Critical to catch low PMF *before* fundraising on PMF.

## Anti-patterns
- **Running it too early.** If you have 10 users and 6 say "very disappointed", that's not statistically meaningful. Wait for ~40+ engaged users.
- **Surveying only happy customers.** Survey everyone active. Bias kills the signal.
- **Treating it as one-time.** PMF can decay. Re-run quarterly.
- **Hitting 40% in a tiny niche and assuming it scales.** Validate that the niche has expansion path (see [[Wedge and Beachhead]]).
- **Ignoring the qualitative answers.** The "what type of person" + "main benefit" answers are the most actionable part of the survey, not the 40% number.

## What to do with the data

- Cluster the "very disappointed" users — write down their common traits, common use cases, common job-to-be-done
- That cluster *is* your ICP. Don't argue with it. Don't try to find a "better" ICP.
- Re-aim marketing, sales, product roadmap toward this cluster.
- The other clusters become deprioritized (not banned, just not prioritized).
