# 7 Powers (Hamilton Helmer)

> A "power" is a condition that creates persistent, differential returns. Without one, your business is a feature waiting to be commoditized. Most companies don't have a power. Most pitches that claim to have one are lying.

## When to use it
- Strategy work: "what makes this business defensible?"
- Investor narrative: every credible Series A+ deck answers "which of the 7 do you have?"
- Quarterly portfolio review: which ventures actually have an emerging power, which don't?
- Premortem: if a competitor copies us, which of the 7 protects us?

## The 7 powers

1. **Scale Economies** — costs decline (or service quality improves) faster than competitors as you grow. Examples: Amazon logistics, Netflix content spend.
2. **Network Economies** — value of the product grows with each new user. Examples: LinkedIn, Visa, Hinge.
3. **Counter-Positioning** — you adopt a business model the incumbent *can't* copy without damaging their existing business. Example: Vanguard vs. active mutual funds.
4. **Switching Costs** — customers face high friction to leaving (data lock-in, retraining, integration). Examples: Salesforce, Oracle, enterprise accounting platforms.
5. **Branding** — customer pays more for the same product because of trust/identity/affect. Examples: Hermès, Apple, Aesop.
6. **Cornered Resource** — preferential access to a scarce input (a person, a license, a piece of IP). Examples: Pixar's talent in '90s, ASML's lithography patents.
7. **Process Power** — proprietary execution method that compounds and is hard to copy even if known. Examples: Toyota Production System, In-N-Out staff training.

A real power must satisfy two tests: **benefit** (creates real value) and **barrier** (prevents arbitrage by competitors).

## How to apply

### Finanshels — what's the emerging power?
- **Scale economies?** Possibly — AI-native ops drives marginal cost of service ↓ as we automate. **Watch.**
- **Network economies?** Weak. Accounting is mostly 1:1.
- **Counter-positioning?** Maybe — traditional UAE accounting firms can't price for SMB without cannibalising their enterprise margins. **Strong candidate.**
- **Switching costs?** Real — books, history, AML records all sit inside our system. **Strong.**
- **Branding?** Building — trust in regulated category is a real branding power.
- **Cornered resource?** No.
- **Process power?** Emerging — CoE methodology + WhatsApp-first sales motion + AI-native QA. **Watch.**

**Net:** Two strong (counter-positioning, switching costs) + two emerging (scale, process). Healthy. The strategic move is to *deepen* counter-positioning by going further down-market than incumbents can rationally follow.

### Biggdate — what's the emerging power?
- **Network economies** are the obvious play (every dating app has them), but they're saturated by Hinge/Bumble/Tinder.
- **Branding** is the asymmetric one — trust + dignity in a category that's mostly cheap.
- **Counter-positioning** — if we build a *paid-only* product, the incumbents can't follow without breaking their ad model.

**Net:** Branding + counter-positioning is the play. Network effects come later, and only if the brand earns them.

### StartupOS — what's the emerging power?
- **Process power** if the operating methodology is the real product.
- **Switching costs** if data + context lock-in becomes meaningful.
- **Cornered resource** if Meet's portfolio companies become a testbed no one else has.

**Net:** Process + switching costs. Network economies very unlikely.

## Anti-patterns
- **Claiming "first-mover advantage" as a power.** It's not — it's a window, not a moat.
- **Claiming "AI" as a power.** It's a capability, not a barrier. Everyone has AI.
- **Confusing brand awareness with branding power.** Branding power = customer pays *more for the same thing*. Awareness alone doesn't do that.
- **Building features instead of powers.** A power is structural. A feature is copyable next quarter.
