# First Principles Thinking

> Break a problem down to its irreducible facts, then reason up from there. Don't reason by analogy ("we'll be the Uber of X") — that imports someone else's solution shape into your problem. Reason from the ground floor.

## When to use it
- A market or industry has obvious "rules" everyone follows — and you suspect they're inherited assumptions, not laws
- Pricing decisions ("the industry charges $X/hour")
- Build vs buy decisions where "everyone uses X" feels suspicious
- When you've been beaten down by status-quo thinking ("that's just how it works in UAE")
- Entrepreneurial moments — the entire premise of most ventures is "this assumption is wrong"

## The process — 3 questions

1. **What am I actually trying to achieve?** Strip away the form to find the function.
2. **What's known for certain at the base level?** Physics, math, regulation, biology, verifiable facts. Not opinions, not industry conventions.
3. **What's the minimum-cost path from the facts to the goal?** Build up without inheriting any "that's just how it's done".

The Musk example: SpaceX rocket pricing. Industry: $65M per launch. First principles: rockets are aluminium, carbon, copper, plus fuel; raw material cost = ~$2M. The 30× gap is *manufacturing inefficiency*, not physics. Therefore reusable rockets become economically rational. Industry's "you can't afford to" was based on inherited assumptions, not first principles.

## How to apply

### Finanshels — first principles on UAE SMB accounting

**Industry assumption:** SMBs need an accountant who visits in person, sends Excel sheets, charges by hour, files quarterly.

**First principles audit:**
- What does the SMB *actually need*? Compliance + clean books + sleep at night.
- What's required by regulation? Specific filings on specific dates; an authorized signatory; AML compliance.
- What's not actually required? Quarterly in-person meetings, paper-based handover, hour-billing, geographically-local accountant.
- What's the minimum-cost path to "compliance + clean books"?

The output is Finanshels — but stripped of inherited rituals. AI-native ops, WhatsApp-first, productized pricing, asynchronous everything.

### StartupOS — first principles on "founder tools"

**Industry assumption:** founders use Notion + Linear + Slack + Calendly + their own google docs.

**First principles audit:**
- What does a founder *actually need* to operate?
  1. Capture (where do thoughts go)
  2. Structure (decisions, priorities, plans)
  3. Context (memory of what was decided, why, by whom)
  4. Execution (delegation, follow-through)
  5. Thinking partner (someone to pressure-test)
- Existing tools serve 1, 2, 4 well. None serve 3 (context) or 5 (thinking partner) well.
- The wedge is in the unmet need, not in being "another Notion competitor".

### Biggdate — first principles on dating apps

**Industry assumption:** swipe, photo-first, freemium, ad-based, algorithmic match volume.

**First principles audit:**
- What does someone seeking a partner *actually need*? Someone real, someone compatible, someone who's also actively looking, someone they can meet.
- What does volume of matches accomplish? Optionality, but at the cost of decision fatigue + commodification.
- What's the minimum-cost path to "real partner found"? Verified intent + meaningful filtering + bias to off-app meeting.
- Conclusion: paid-only, verified-first, low-volume-high-quality is a coherent first-principles design — not "premium freemium tier".

### Personal — first principles on operating cadence

**Industry assumption:** founders should focus, do one thing, calendar-block 4 hours of deep work daily, hire fast, fundraise to scale.

**First principles audit (for Meet specifically):**
- What does Meet actually need to compound? Leverage across multiple bets that teach each other; revenue from at least one to fund the others; public credibility for distribution and capital.
- Is "one venture" a law or a convention? Convention.
- Conclusion: Meet's portfolio approach is *first-principles correct for his situation*, even though it violates the "focus" convention. The relevant constraint isn't "number of ventures" but "do they teach each other and feed back into one another's compounding".

## Anti-patterns
- **First principles as superficial vocabulary.** Saying "let's go first principles" while still reasoning by analogy = theater.
- **Re-deriving everything when conventions actually encode good reasons.** First principles isn't anti-tradition. Some conventions exist because the physics actually requires them. Verify by attempting to derive — sometimes the convention wins.
- **Confusing first principles with contrarianism.** Being different isn't the goal. Being correct from base reasoning is.
- **Reasoning from incomplete base facts.** First principles requires you actually know the base facts. If you don't know the regulation, the chemistry, the unit economics — you'll derive nonsense.
- **Skipping the conventional wisdom audit.** Sometimes the right move is convention. First principles means *checking* — not automatically rejecting.

## Practice exercise (worth doing for any new venture decision)

For 30 minutes, treat the assumed answer as suspicious and:
1. Write what you're trying to achieve in plain language.
2. Write what's *actually required* (regulation, physics, math).
3. Write what's *conventionally done* but might not be required.
4. Write the minimum-cost path from #1 to #2.
5. Compare to the conventional path. Where do they diverge?

The divergence is often where the moat hides.
