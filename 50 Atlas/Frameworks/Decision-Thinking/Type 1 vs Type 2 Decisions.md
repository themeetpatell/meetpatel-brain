# Type 1 vs Type 2 Decisions (Bezos)

> Most decisions are reversible. Treat them as such — decide fast, ship, course-correct. Only the irreversible ones deserve deep deliberation. Most founders waste their best thinking time on Type 2 decisions and rush the Type 1s. Reverse this.

## When to use it
- Before any decision — first classify it
- When deliberation has gone on more than 1 day and the decision is two-way-door
- When you're tempted to "just decide quickly" on something that's actually one-way-door
- Teaching team members to operate with appropriate speed
- Founder bandwidth allocation — your best thinking time is finite

## The framework — two types

### Type 1 — One-way door (irreversible)
- Walking through changes the landscape; you can't come back
- Examples: selling the company, firing a co-founder, public brand promise, taking strategic-investor money, naming the venture, pricing model adopted publicly
- **Approach:** Slow. Deliberate. Premortem. Multiple advisors. Decision frame in writing. Sleep on it. Maybe a week.

### Type 2 — Two-way door (reversible)
- You can walk back through if the new room isn't right
- Examples: trying a new pricing experiment, hiring a junior contractor, posting a single LinkedIn post, running a 2-week marketing test, scheduling a meeting
- **Approach:** Fast. Bias to action. Often best made by the person closest to the work, not escalated up.

### Critical insight
- **80–90% of decisions are Type 2.** Treat them as such.
- The bigger problem in most orgs is *Type 2 decisions being made too slowly*, not Type 1 decisions being made too quickly.

## How to apply

### Finanshels — classify recurring decisions

| Decision | Type | Speed |
|---|---|---|
| Hire a junior pre-sales analyst | **Type 2** (can let them go in probation) | Fast — function head decides |
| Hire a CFO-level | **Type 1** (firing senior people is costly) | Slow — Meet decides, premortem first |
| Test a new pricing tier | **Type 2** (can change next month) | Fast — just ship a test |
| Public pricing model (e.g., "we are forever 50% cheaper than competitors") | **Type 1** | Slow |
| Sign a 3-year channel partner exclusive | **Type 1** | Slow |
| Try a new WhatsApp opener | **Type 2** | Fast |
| Switch BSP provider | **Type 1.5** (reversible but painful) | Medium |
| Public claim of AI accuracy | **Type 1** (regulatory + brand) | Slow |
| Run a one-month content campaign | **Type 2** | Fast |
| Adopt or change company core values | **Type 1** | Slow |

### Cross-portfolio

Most of Meet's productive output is Type 2 decisions made fast.

Most of Meet's biggest leverage is the small number of Type 1 decisions, made *carefully*:
- Which wedge to lock at StartupOS (Type 1 — committing months of build)
- Whether to fundraise (Type 1 — equity + investor + board obligations are forever)
- Whether to kill a venture (Type 1 — closes a door)
- Public brand promises (Type 1)
- Major hire (Type 1 for senior; Type 2 for junior)

### The hybrid case — Type 1.5

Some decisions are reversible but expensive to reverse. Treat as closer to Type 1 in deliberation, closer to Type 2 in speed.

Examples:
- Software migration (reversible but painful, take 2 weeks not 6 months to decide)
- Office space lease (reversible but with cost)
- Vendor selection for non-critical functions

## How to use this with the team

Build a culture vocabulary: when someone asks "should we do X?", first response is: *"Is this Type 1 or Type 2?"*

- If Type 2: "Then why are we discussing for more than 10 minutes? Decide and ship."
- If Type 1: "Then let's run a decision frame and a premortem. Don't decide today."

This single discipline saves 30% of meeting time in most orgs.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating every decision as Type 1.** Cripples operating speed. Most common in risk-averse cultures.
- **Treating every decision as Type 2.** Catastrophic for the actual Type 1 decisions. Most common in "move fast and break things" cultures.
- **Misclassifying.** Some decisions feel reversible but aren't (firing a key employee, public commitments). Some feel irreversible but are (most "we already invested too much" sunk-cost decisions are actually reversible).
- **Outsourcing Type 1 decisions to consensus.** Type 1 decisions need *a* decider (use [[RAPID]]). Consensus on Type 1 decisions is mediocrity insurance.
