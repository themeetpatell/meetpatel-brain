# MEDDIC — Sales Qualification

> Six letters that separate real deals from polite pipeline fluff. Designed for enterprise sales but works in SMB too — actually, *especially* in SMB where you can't afford to chase ghost deals.

## When to use it
- Qualifying any new lead with meaningful deal size
- Sales pipeline reviews — every deal in the funnel should have a MEDDIC score
- Discovery calls — the questions to ask
- Deal review: "why is this deal stuck?" almost always means a MEDDIC component is missing

## The framework — 6 components

1. **Metrics** — what quantifiable outcome does the customer want? ROI, time saved, error reduction, revenue lift. If you can't name the number, the deal has no anchor.

2. **Economic Buyer** — who actually signs the cheque? Not the user, not the champion — the person with budget authority. In SMB, often the owner. In enterprise, often hidden.

3. **Decision Criteria** — how will they decide? Features they care about, integrations required, compliance/security needs. If you don't know, you're selling blind.

4. **Decision Process** — the path from "interested" to "signed". Who needs to approve? What's the timeline? Procurement involved? Legal? Board?

5. **Identify Pain** — what's broken today, concretely? Pain is the engine. Without pain, no urgency. Without urgency, no deal.

6. **Champion** — is there someone *inside* the org who actively wants you to win? A champion is not a fan — a champion *takes risk* to advance your deal internally.

(Some versions add a 7th C: **Competition** — what alternatives are they evaluating? Including "do nothing".)

## How to apply

### Finanshels — MEDDIC for SMB accounting deal

- **Metrics:** "Save 10 hrs/week on bookkeeping" / "Never miss VAT deadline" / "Get clean books by month-5 of FY"
- **Economic Buyer:** Owner-founder (almost always)
- **Decision Criteria:** UAE compliance expertise, WhatsApp responsiveness, transparent pricing, ability to handle their specific industry (F&B / retail / trading)
- **Decision Process:** Owner decides solo or with spouse/co-founder. 1–3 week cycle typical. No procurement.
- **Identify Pain:** Recent missed filing / tax notice / disappointed by current accountant / scaling past first finance hire
- **Champion:** Often the owner themselves; sometimes a junior accountant or ops manager who pushes the change

**Bad deal signal:** owner says "interested but let me think" with no specific pain or metric. **Kill or nurture lightly.**

### StartupOS — MEDDIC for founder deal

- **Metrics:** "Make better decisions faster" → translates to specific (decisions per week, time per decision)
- **Economic Buyer:** Founder (usually)
- **Decision Criteria:** Integration with existing stack (Notion, Linear), AI quality, voice/personality fit
- **Decision Process:** Founder tries free → personal commitment → org rollout (or stays personal tool)
- **Identify Pain:** Specific recent moment of decision regret / overwhelm / lack of thinking partner
- **Champion:** Founder is buyer, champion, and user. Single-threaded — simpler MEDDIC.

### Cross-venture rule
- **Tier A deal:** all 6 MEDDIC components mapped + pain is acute. Worth deep sales time.
- **Tier B deal:** 4–5 components mapped, some uncertainty. Nurture, qualify further.
- **Tier C deal:** <4 components mapped. Polite "let's reconnect when [trigger event]" — don't sink time.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating "interested" as a qualification.** Interest is noise. MEDDIC is signal.
- **Skipping Economic Buyer.** Most deals die because you're talking to the influencer, not the buyer.
- **Confusing user with champion.** A user who likes your product but won't risk political capital for you is not a champion.
- **Asking MEDDIC questions all at once.** Spread across 2–3 calls. Interrogating kills rapport.
- **No competition mapping.** Even if they say "we're only talking to you", they're comparing you to *the status quo*. That's competition.
