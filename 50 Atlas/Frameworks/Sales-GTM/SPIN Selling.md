# SPIN Selling

> The order in which you ask questions in a sales conversation matters more than the questions themselves. SPIN gives you the right sequence: Situation → Problem → Implication → Need-payoff. Built from analyzing 35,000+ real sales calls.

## When to use it
- Discovery calls
- High-consideration sales (SaaS, services, anything >$5k)
- When prospects are aware of a pain but haven't yet quantified its cost
- Coaching anyone on your sales team — SPIN is the most teachable sales discipline

## The framework — 4 question types, in this order

### S — Situation Questions
Establish context. Use sparingly — too many feel like an interrogation.
- *"How many staff do you have today?"*
- *"What accounting software are you using?"*
- *"How long have you been operating in UAE?"*

**Goal:** enough context to ask good Problem questions. **2–4 max.**

### P — Problem Questions
Surface specific dissatisfactions.
- *"What's the most frustrating part of your current setup?"*
- *"When was the last time you missed a filing deadline?"*
- *"What part of running accounting takes more time than you'd like?"*

**Goal:** get them to name a real problem in their own words. Don't suggest the problem — let them surface it.

### I — Implication Questions
Expand the cost of the problem. This is the most under-used and most powerful set.
- *"What happens when you miss a filing deadline?"*
- *"How much time does that lost weekend cost you in revenue work?"*
- *"What does that mean for your team's confidence in the numbers?"*
- *"If this kept happening for another year, what would the cumulative cost be?"*

**Goal:** the buyer should *feel* the cost of the status quo — not just intellectually agree the problem exists. Implication questions create urgency.

### N — Need-payoff Questions
Make them articulate the value of solving the problem.
- *"If you never missed a filing again, what would change for you?"*
- *"What would it mean to get those 10 hours/week back?"*
- *"If your books were always investor-ready, how would that change your fundraise timeline?"*

**Goal:** the buyer should sell themselves on the value of the solution, using their own language. Then your pitch is them quoting themselves.

## How to apply

### Finanshels — SPIN-structured discovery call

**S (situation):** *"How many staff today? What software? UAE entity or free zone?"*

**P (problem):** *"What's the most painful part of compliance for you right now?"*

**I (implication):**
- *"What happens when AML filing slips?"*
- *"How does that affect your relationship with your bank?"*
- *"If that pattern continues for another quarter, what's the downstream effect?"*

**N (need-payoff):**
- *"If you had complete confidence your filings were always on time, what would that free up for you?"*
- *"What would 'clean books at month-end every month' actually let you do that you can't today?"*

When they answer the N questions, *that's* your pitch — repeat it back to them.

### Biggdate — SPIN for premium-paid sign-up

**S:** *"How long have you been actively dating? Which apps?"*

**P:** *"What's the most frustrating part of dating apps for you right now?"*

**I:**
- *"What does spending 90 minutes a day swiping cost you?"*
- *"How does the volume of low-quality matches affect how you feel about dating overall?"*

**N:**
- *"If you matched with 3 thoughtful, verified people per month instead of 100 random profiles, what would that change?"*

## Anti-patterns
- **Skipping Implication questions.** Most reps go S → P → N and wonder why deals don't close. Implication is the ignition.
- **Asking Implication questions as gotchas.** They have to come from genuine curiosity, not "let me lead you to the answer I want".
- **Too many Situation questions.** Feels like a survey. The prospect disengages.
- **Asking Need-payoff before the problem is fully felt.** "If you had a perfect solution, would you buy?" with no felt cost = a hypothetical. Wasted.
- **Treating SPIN as a script.** It's a sequence, not a script. Adapt the questions to what they just said.
