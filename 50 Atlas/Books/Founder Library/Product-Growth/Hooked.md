# Hooked — Nir Eyal

## Why it matters to Meet

The definitive product-design book on habit formation. Eyal's 4-step "Hook Model" (Trigger → Action → Variable Reward → Investment) explains why some products become daily rituals and others get deleted. Powerful for Biggdate (consumer engagement), partially relevant to Finanshels (dashboard return-visits), and important for StartupOS (whether founders actually use it weekly). Eyal himself later wrote *Indistractable* as a counterweight — that companion is essential reading on the ethics of using Hooked patterns.

## One-line thesis

Habit-forming products run through a 4-step loop. Each pass through the loop strengthens the user's automatic association with your product. Master the loop, and your product becomes part of the user's routine — without ongoing marketing spend.

## Core ideas

1. **The Hook Model — 4 phases.** Trigger (external first, then internal) → Action (the simplest behavior in anticipation of reward) → Variable Reward (unpredictable payoff) → Investment (user does something that increases future value of the product). Full framework note at [[Product-Growth/Hooked Model|Hooked Model framework]].

2. **Internal triggers are the goal.** External triggers (notifications, ads) get the user started. Internal triggers (emotions, contexts) sustain the habit. The shift from external to internal trigger = habit formation succeeded. "I check Instagram when I'm bored" is an internal trigger.

3. **Variability is the magic.** Predictable rewards become boring quickly. Variable rewards (you don't know exactly what you'll get) sustain engagement. Slot machines, social feeds, dating apps all exploit this.

4. **The Investment phase is what creates the loop.** When the user invests (uploads data, adds friends, builds reputation), the *next* trigger becomes more compelling because the product is now uniquely valuable to them.

5. **Three types of Variable Reward.**
   - *Tribe* (social validation — likes, comments)
   - *Hunt* (resource gathering — info, opportunities, matches)
   - *Self* (personal mastery — completion, skill growth)
   
   Best products tap multiple types.

6. **The Manipulation Matrix.** Eyal's ethics check: is this product something you'd use yourself? Is it materially improving the user's life? If neither, you're building something exploitative. Build accordingly.

## How to apply

### Biggdate (use with caution — dating apps + Hook = ethics question)

- **Trigger:** A predictable daily moment (e.g., 8am UAE — curated match drop). Internal trigger develops: "morning coffee + check today's Biggdate match".
- **Action:** Single tap to view today's curated profile.
- **Variable Reward:** *Quality* of today's match varies. Sometimes amazing fit, sometimes interesting-not-perfect. Tribe + Hunt categories both engaged.
- **Investment:** Profile depth, interaction history, intent signals — each adds future value. Three months in, the user has *invested* enough that switching = losing real value.

**Ethics gate:** the goal must be *real outcomes* (off-app dates, relationships) not *engagement metrics* (time-in-app, swipes). The moment Biggdate optimizes for engagement at the cost of outcomes, it becomes Tinder. Don't.

### StartupOS — Hook model for retention

- **Trigger:** Monday morning external email — "what's your top priority this week?". Becomes internal: "Monday = open StartupOS".
- **Action:** Open the OS, set the priority.
- **Variable Reward:** AI surfaces context, recalls prior decisions, drafts the first move. Reward is *clarity gained*.
- **Investment:** Every decision logged, every priority captured, every reflection — compounds. The OS becomes valuable in a way no other tool is.

**Ethical alignment:** StartupOS's habit is *good for the founder*. The investment phase isn't extractive — it's compounding. Aligned.

### Finanshels — limited Hook model applicability

Most Finanshels usage is transactional + periodic (filings, statements) — not daily-habit. Limited Hook application. Where it matters:
- The customer dashboard should reward return visits with *new* insights, *new* surfaced opportunities — variable rewards. Not just "here's your same data".
- The WhatsApp interface should occasionally surprise with proactive value (e.g., "you're due for AML refresh in 30 days — want me to start it?"). Variable, unexpected, useful.

### Personal — Meet's own habit design

- The daily-note habit Meet is trying to build IS a Hook design challenge:
  - Trigger: morning coffee
  - Action: open vault, type 3 lines
  - Variable Reward: insight surfaces, weekly review feels richer
  - Investment: the vault gets more useful as the daily notes accumulate

## Where the book is wrong (or limited)

- Some critics (rightly) argue the book glamorizes manipulation. Eyal later acknowledged this and wrote *Indistractable* as the counterweight. Read both. Don't read Hooked without Indistractable.
- The model works best for digital products with frequent use cycles. Less applicable to high-consideration, low-frequency products (Finanshels accounting — once-a-month is the natural cadence).
- The "Investment" phase can become *lock-in* if abused. Lock-in feels good short-term but generates anti-fan customers long-term.
- The variable reward principle is real but exhausting if overused — apps that lean too hard on it become slot machines and burn out users.
