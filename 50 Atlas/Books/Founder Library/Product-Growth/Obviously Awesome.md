# Obviously Awesome — April Dunford

## Why it matters to Meet

The best book ever written on *positioning*. Dunford spent two decades positioning B2B software companies for acquisition or growth. Her core point: positioning is not branding, not messaging, not taglines — it's the *strategic decision* about what category to be in, who you serve, and what unique value you create. Most products fail not because they're bad but because they're positioned in the wrong category. Critical for StartupOS (potentially new category), Biggdate (re-positioning within dating), and even Finanshels (the trust-led AI-native angle).

## One-line thesis

Positioning is choosing the context in which your product is seen. Get the context right and your strengths look obvious. Get it wrong and even excellent products look mediocre.

## Core ideas

1. **The 5 + 1 components of positioning.**
   - **Competitive alternatives** — what customers would use if you didn't exist (be honest — "spreadsheets" or "doing nothing" often counts)
   - **Unique attributes** — what you have that the alternatives don't (must be verifiable, not aspirational)
   - **Value (so what)** — what those unique attributes enable for the customer
   - **Best-fit customers** — who has the strongest reason to value that value
   - **Market category** — the frame of reference that makes your strengths look strongest
   - **(+1) Relevant trends** — broader currents that make this positioning *more* compelling now

2. **Positioning is a choice.** Same product can be positioned in multiple categories. The right category is the one where you have an unfair advantage AND where customers experience the most pain AND where the comparison highlights your strengths. Pick deliberately.

3. **Re-positioning is normal and necessary.** Especially after pivots, market shifts, new competitor moves. Companies that fail to re-position become stale. Healthy companies re-evaluate positioning annually.

4. **The "you are not your customer" rule.** Founders position based on what they think is interesting. Customers value different things. Positioning research = customer research.

5. **Categories have a Frame of Reference.** When a customer hears "we're a [category]", they immediately bring expectations: pricing, features, integrations, who else is in the category. Choose the category that brings *helpful* expectations.

## Killer quotes

> *"Weak positioning leaves customers confused, sales teams struggling, and marketing burning cycles."*

> *"You are not stuck with the positioning you started with. You can change it. Just understand that you have to actively choose it."*

> *"The market category you decide to compete in is the single most important context-setting choice you can make."*

> *"If the answer to 'who is our best-fit customer' is 'everyone', you have no positioning."*

## How to apply

### Finanshels — positioning audit

Apply Dunford's 5+1:

- **Competitive alternatives:** traditional UAE accounting firms; in-house bookkeeper; Khatabook + Tally combo; "doing nothing until VAT panic"
- **Unique attributes:** AI-native execution + WhatsApp-first + UAE regulatory expertise + transparent pricing
- **Value (so what):** *peace of mind* — owner doesn't think about compliance, books are clean, filings on time, regulator stays away
- **Best-fit customers:** UAE SMBs $200k–$2M revenue, owner-operator, post-VAT-implementation, frustrated with slow/opaque incumbents
- **Market category:** *"AI-native accounting platform for UAE SMBs"* (current) or *"Trusted SMB financial operations partner"* (alternative)
- **+1 Trend:** AI commoditization makes the operator-led version more credible (not just "another AI tool")

**Choice to make:** Category framing matters. "AI accounting" attracts comparison to other AI plays. "SMB financial operations partner" attracts comparison to incumbents (where Finanshels' AI-native edge stands out more). Test both.

### StartupOS — positioning audit (pre-launch)

- **Competitive alternatives:** Notion + Linear + manual workflow; "winging it"; expensive ($5k+/mo) Chief of Staff hire; founder community + Slack
- **Unique attributes:** AI cofounder that operates *with* the founder; context-aware across decisions; persistent memory
- **Value:** clarity, speed, fewer decision-regrets, structured thinking
- **Best-fit customers:** First-time + second-time founders, technical, 0–10 employees, comfortable with AI tools
- **Market category:** *"Founder Operating System"* (own a new category — see [[Strategy/Play Bigger|Play Bigger]])
- **+1 Trend:** AI agents maturing from novelty to operational tools; founders increasingly working solo or 2–3 person teams

### Biggdate — positioning audit

- **Competitive alternatives:** Hinge, Bumble, Tinder, Aisle, Boo, Hily; matchmakers; offline events; "give up"
- **Unique attributes:** trust-led onboarding; intent-verified members; paid-only; deeper compatibility framework
- **Value:** *real shot at a long-term partner* without the dating-app exhaustion
- **Best-fit customers:** 25–40, professionals, NRI + UAE-based, exhausted by swipe apps, willing to pay for quality
- **Market category:** *"Intentional dating"* / *"Relationship intelligence"* / *"Premium long-term-focused dating"* (test category framing)
- **+1 Trend:** dating app fatigue is a cultural moment; "back to relationship seriousness" is in cultural air

### Cross-portfolio rule

- Every venture should have a documented Positioning Statement using Dunford's structure
- File at `30 Ventures/[Venture]/Notes/Positioning.md`
- Re-evaluate annually OR when a competitor moves significantly OR when sales objections cluster around "we don't really understand what you are"

## Where the book is wrong (or limited)

- The book is heavily B2B SaaS. Consumer positioning (Biggdate, MealVerse) requires translation — but the principles hold.
- Dunford assumes you have customer research data. Pre-launch ventures don't. The framework still applies but with more hypothesis + iteration.
- Light on *category creation* itself (Play Bigger is the companion). Dunford is excellent within an existing or adjacent category; for *new* categories, pair with Play Bigger.
- Some chapters can read as "positioning consultancy methodology" — skim those for the operating principles.
