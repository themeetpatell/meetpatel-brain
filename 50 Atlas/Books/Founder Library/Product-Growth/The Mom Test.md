# The Mom Test — Rob Fitzpatrick

## Why it matters to Meet

The shortest, most useful book on customer development ever written. Fitzpatrick's central insight: people lie politely about your idea, especially people who like you. The Mom Test is a set of interview rules that surface truth — by talking about the user's *past behavior* instead of their *future opinions*. Meet should treat this as a foundational manual for every venture's early customer-dev work — Biggdate, StartupOS, and any future venture.

## One-line thesis

Don't ask people if they like your idea. Ask them about their life. Specifically, ask about specific past events — because past behavior is the only honest signal in customer development.

## Core ideas

1. **The Mom Test (the rule).** A good question is one your mom couldn't lie about. "Would you use this app?" — your mom would lie. "When was the last time you tried to solve [problem]? Walk me through what happened" — your mom couldn't lie even if she wanted to, because she's describing a real event.

2. **Three rules of customer conversation.**
   - Talk about their life, not your idea
   - Ask about specifics in the past, not generic opinions about the future
   - Talk less, listen more

3. **Compliments are useless.** "I love your idea!" tells you nothing. Compliments often signal that the conversation has gone wrong — you've slipped into pitch mode.

4. **Fluff is the enemy.** Fluff = generalities, future-tense, hypothetical. "I'd definitely use that" / "Sounds great" / "I'd probably pay for it" — all fluff. Re-direct to specifics.

5. **Commitment and advancement.** The only real signal that a conversation went well: did the prospect *commit* something (time, introductions, money, data)? Did they *advance* the relationship (next meeting, intro to decision-maker)? Compliments are not commitment.

6. **The three killer questions.**
   - *"How are you dealing with this now?"* (uncovers real workarounds and budget)
   - *"What's the hardest part about [problem]?"* (forces specifics)
   - *"Tell me about the last time [event happened]"* (gets you in the actual moment)

## How to apply

### Biggdate — Mom Test interview script (pre-launch)

Use [[_AI/Prompts/personal/journal-extract|the Mom Test framework note]] for the discipline. Sample questions for a Biggdate ICP interview:

❌ Don't ask: *"Would you use a high-intent dating app?"*

✅ Ask:
- *"Tell me about your last 3 months on dating apps. Which ones, in what order, why did you leave or keep using them?"*
- *"When was the last time you went on a date from an app? Walk me through it."*
- *"How long did you typically stay on each app before deleting?"*
- *"How much have you spent on premium dating subscriptions in the last year?"*
- *"Have you ever paid for anything *adjacent* — matchmaking services, coaching, in-person events?"*

The answers tell you the real pain, real spending, real switching behavior.

### StartupOS — Mom Test for founder interviews

❌ Don't ask: *"Would you want an AI Chief of Staff?"*

✅ Ask:
- *"Tell me about the last major decision you made as a founder. What was the moment, who did you talk to, what tools did you use?"*
- *"What's the hardest part about operating without a co-founder?"*
- *"When was the last time you wished you had a thinking partner? What did you actually do?"*
- *"Walk me through how you decided your last hire."*

### Finanshels — for re-positioning conversations

Even with existing Finanshels customers, the Mom Test is powerful. To validate pricing or new service lines:

❌ *"Would you pay $X for an AI-bookkeeping add-on?"*

✅ *"Tell me about the last month-end close. What slowed you down? Who fixed it?"*

### Cross-venture rule

Every venture should run 15–20 Mom Test interviews *before* committing to a major pivot, new wedge, or major feature. The output: a synthesis of pain language, workarounds, money already spent, and trigger events. File at `30 Ventures/[Venture]/Notes/Customer Interviews/`.

## Where the book is wrong (or limited)

- The book is short (~150 pages). Reads fast. The brevity is mostly virtue but occasionally leaves nuance out.
- Fitzpatrick's frame is mostly B2B — for consumer products (Biggdate, MealVerse), the discipline transfers but the questions need adapting.
- Light on *who* to talk to. Pair with Bullseye Framework and ICP work for the segment selection.
- Less useful late-stage — at 1,000 customers, surveys + analytics > interviews. Mom Test is for the first ~50 conversations.
