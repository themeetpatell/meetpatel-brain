# Lean Analytics — Alistair Croll & Benjamin Yoskovitz

## Why it matters to Meet

The most rigorous book on what to measure when — at which stage of the company, for which business model, to make which decisions. Most founders pick metrics from the airport-bookshop business books (NPS, MRR, MAU) without thinking about whether those metrics actually help them decide anything. Lean Analytics gives you the stage-by-stage map: the *One Metric That Matters* (OMTM) and how it changes as you grow.

## One-line thesis

At every stage of a company's growth, there's exactly ONE metric that matters more than every other. Pick it deliberately. Move it relentlessly. Ignore everything else until that metric is healthy.

## Core ideas

1. **The One Metric That Matters (OMTM).** At any given moment, one number should drive every team conversation. Different stage, different OMTM. Many founders track too many metrics — which means they make no metric-driven decisions.

2. **The 5 stages of company growth.** Empathy → Stickiness → Virality → Revenue → Scale. Each stage has its own OMTM and goals. Trying to optimize Stage 4 metrics while in Stage 1 = wasted effort.

3. **Six business model archetypes.** SaaS, eCommerce, Mobile App, Two-sided Marketplace, Media, User-Generated Content. Each has its own canonical metrics + danger zones. Don't apply SaaS metrics to a marketplace, etc.

4. **Vanity vs Actionable metrics.** Vanity = makes you feel good (cumulative signups, page views). Actionable = changes behavior (conversion rate, time-to-aha, retention curve). Most dashboards over-weight vanity.

5. **The Line in the Sand.** Set a *specific target* for the OMTM before you start optimizing. "We will hit 40% week-1 retention by end of Q3." Without a target, you can't tell when you're done.

6. **Benchmarks per archetype.** The book lists ranges for healthy metrics per business model — e.g., SaaS month-1 churn under 5%, marketplace take rate 15–30%, etc. These give you reference points so you know whether your number is good or terrible.

## Killer quotes

> *"The right metric will make you take action. Anything else is a vanity metric."*

> *"You should have ONE metric you're focused on. Picking that metric is half the battle."*

> *"The only line in the sand that matters is the one you draw yourself."*

> *"Lean Analytics is about asking the most important question for your company, right now, and getting an answer fast."*

## How to apply

### Finanshels — OMTM by stage

- **Current stage:** Revenue → moving toward Scale
- **OMTM candidate:** *Monthly Active Compliant Accounts* (the customers who are realizing the value they hired Finanshels for — clean books + on-time filings + AML good standing)
- Why this beats vanity alternatives: tracks *value delivered*, not just revenue. A churning customer with paid invoices is a problem the revenue number hides. Compliant Accounts surfaces it.
- **Line in the sand:** specific quarterly target on Compliant Accounts growth + churn rate.
- **Supporting metrics:** Acquisition (new accounts/month), Activation (% reaching compliant status by month 2), Retention (Compliant Accounts QoQ).

### StartupOS — OMTM pre-launch + early launch

- **Stage:** Empathy → moving toward Stickiness
- **OMTM in Empathy stage:** *Number of validated pain interviews* (target: 25 founders confirm the wedge pain in their words)
- **OMTM when Stickiness stage begins (post-launch):** *Week-1 retention of beta founders* (do they come back?)
- **Line in the sand:** 40% week-1 retention before we worry about anything else
- This discipline prevents the classic StartupOS mistake: pouring effort into acquisition before retention is proven.

### Biggdate — OMTM pre-launch + early launch

- **Stage pre-launch:** Empathy → Stickiness validation
- **OMTM in launch period:** *Weekly Active Verified Members reporting at least one mutual high-intent match*
- **Line in the sand:** 30% W1 retention of paying members before scaling marketing
- **Critical:** don't measure "sign-ups" or "DAU" — these are vanity for a dating app. Measure outcomes that customers paid for.

### Cross-venture rule

- At each quarterly portfolio review, name each venture's current OMTM explicitly
- If a venture's OMTM has been the same for 3+ quarters with no movement, something's broken — either the metric or the work
- Refuse to discuss "dashboards" without identifying the OMTM first

## Where the book is wrong (or limited)

- The "One Metric That Matters" framing can be over-applied. At later stages, multiple metrics genuinely matter. The OMTM discipline is best in early-to-mid growth.
- The benchmarks in the book are dated (2013) and skew US-tech. UAE/GCC benchmarks for SMB SaaS are not in the book — Meet has to build local benchmarks from the Finanshels data itself.
- Heavy on tactical metric design; lighter on the strategic question of *what business are we in*. Pair with [[Strategy/Crossing the Chasm|Crossing the Chasm]] and [[Strategy/7 Powers|7 Powers]] for that.
- The "Lean" methodology assumes rapid iteration. In regulated categories (Finanshels), you can't iterate weekly on customer experience — some experiments take quarters.
