# Amp It Up — Frank Slootman

## Why it matters to Meet

Slootman built three multi-billion-dollar companies (Data Domain, ServiceNow, Snowflake). His thesis is unfashionable but correct: most companies underperform because they operate at half intensity, with bloated bureaucracy, generic missions, and tolerance for mediocrity. "Amping it up" means raising standards, sharpening focus, and refusing to accept slow as normal. Meet runs a portfolio where this discipline directly applies — Finanshels especially benefits from the operating intensity Slootman describes.

## One-line thesis

Most companies are 30% as fast as they could be. The job of the CEO is to amp the intensity — raise standards, sharpen narratives, narrow focus, prune the middle — until output matches what the team is actually capable of.

## Core ideas

1. **Raise standards.** Most teams operate at the standards their CEO accepts. If the CEO accepts mediocre work, mediocre work continues. Slootman's standard: "is this great or not?" — and acting on the answer.

2. **Narrow focus.** Most strategy decks list too many priorities. Slootman ruthlessly narrows. "If we have ten priorities, we have none." Pick three. Maybe one. Then own that pick.

3. **Sharpen narrative.** The story leadership tells about the company should be sharp, specific, and repeatable. If the team can't tell the company narrative in one sentence, leadership has failed.

4. **Prune the middle.** Bureaucracy creeps in because layers add. Slootman cuts middle management aggressively. Flat structures + clear ownership + visible accountability.

5. **Strive for greatness.** Slootman is openly impatient with people who optimize for comfort. "Effort and quality are within everyone's control. Talent is not. Stop accepting low effort and low quality. Talent will sort itself out."

6. **Pace is a strategy.** Speed of decision-making, speed of shipping, speed of feedback loops. Each compounds. Slow companies stay slow not because they have to — because the CEO doesn't fight it.

## Killer quotes

> *"You don't get to greatness by being good. You get there by being great."*

> *"If you have ten priorities, you have none."*

> *"Drive a wedge between greatness and mediocrity by raising standards and refusing to accept anything else."*

> *"Why are you doing this for years and not seeing the results you want? Probably because the standards you set are too low."*

## How to apply

### Finanshels — what Amp-It-Up would look like

- **Standards audit:** are L10s ending with decisive IDS outcomes, or with vague "let's discuss next week"? Are SOPs being followed or quietly drifted from? Are pre-sales response times hitting the bar, or "close enough"?
- **Priority narrowing:** the current Finanshels strategic priorities (per CLAUDE.md) are AI demand engine, EOS maturity, monetization, productization. Slootman's challenge: pick *one* as the must-ship for this quarter. The others continue but don't take Meet's prime attention.
- **Narrative sharpening:** "Finanshels is the trust-led AI-native SMB accounting platform that handles UAE compliance so owners can run their business." Test if the team can repeat it.

### StartupOS — Slootman's voice on the wedge

- "Pick one wedge. Stop discussing. Ship it. The other three aren't priorities until the first one works." This is the Slootman position. It's right.
- Once chosen, the standards conversation: "is the v1 demo *great* or just *okay*?" Don't ship "okay".

### Meet's personal portfolio

- The portfolio approach (8 ventures) is the opposite of Slootman's "narrow focus" advice. But Meet's defensible position: the ventures *teach* each other and the meta-IP compounds across StartupOS. That's a valid frame Slootman doesn't account for.
- Where Slootman applies even to the portfolio: *within* each venture, narrow ruthlessly. Within Finanshels, pick one strategic priority. Within StartupOS, pick one wedge. The portfolio width doesn't relieve the focus discipline at the venture level.

## Where the book is wrong (or limited)

- Slootman's pace works for enterprise SaaS where there's an existing market to attack. For early venture creation (StartupOS, Biggdate pre-launch), some of the "amp it up" advice misfires — you can't amp the pace of a market you're still discovering.
- The book under-discusses *psychological cost* of operating at his intensity. Sustainable for some founders; burnout-inducing for others. Meet should calibrate to his own engine, not Slootman's.
- Limited on strategy *content* — long on intensity. Pair with [[Strategy/7 Powers|7 Powers]] and [[Strategy/Crossing the Chasm|Crossing the Chasm]] for the *what to amp* question.
