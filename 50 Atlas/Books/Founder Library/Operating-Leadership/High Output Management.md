# High Output Management — Andy Grove

## Why it matters to Meet

The greatest book on operational management ever written. Grove ran Intel through three reinventions (memory chips → microprocessors → architecture licensing). The book is dense, technical, and unfashionably operational — exactly why it remains the canonical reference. If Meet operates the Finanshels CoE on Grove's principles, the CoE compounds. If not, it stays small.

## One-line thesis

A manager's output equals the output of their organization plus the output of neighboring organizations under their influence. Therefore: focus relentlessly on activities that multiply across the team, not on individual contribution.

## Core ideas

1. **Manager's leverage.** Activities are valuable in proportion to how many people they multiply. Hiring well, training, 1:1s, decision-making in groups — high leverage. Doing the work yourself — low leverage. Most managers spend too much time on low-leverage activities.

2. **The 1:1 is the highest-leverage meeting on earth.** Weekly, structured, owned by the report (not the manager). 30–60 minutes. Manager listens 80% of the time. The 1:1 is where information flows that no other meeting surfaces. Skipping = malpractice.

3. **Output metrics + indicators.** Don't measure activity (hours worked, meetings held). Measure *output* (units shipped, deals closed) and *indicators* (leading signals that predict output). Grove pioneered the dual-metric system that became OKRs.

4. **The Breakfast Factory.** Grove uses a breakfast restaurant metaphor to teach operations. Every operation is a production process with inputs, throughput, bottlenecks, quality controls. Apply the production-engineering mindset to knowledge work — radically.

5. **The Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM) model.** Manage people based on their TRM for the *specific task*, not their overall seniority. Low TRM = directive ("here's how to do it"). High TRM = delegation ("here's the outcome — figure it out"). Match style to TRM, or you'll either micromanage seniors or abandon juniors.

6. **Decision-making process.** Free discussion → clear decision → full support. Grove's rule: arguments before the decision (encouraged); arguments after the decision (forbidden). Disagree and commit, before "disagree and commit" became Amazon's slogan.

7. **Managerial Time as currency.** Your time is the budget. Allocating to a meeting is buying the meeting at the cost of everything else you could have done with that hour. Audit your calendar like a CFO.

## Killer quotes

## How to apply

### Finanshels CoE — Grove-mode operating

**Mandatory adoptions:**
- **Weekly 1:1s** with every CoE direct report. Structured agenda (report-owned), 30 min minimum, no skipping without explicit reschedule.
- **Output + Indicator metrics** on the CoE scorecard. Output = compliant accounts maintained, filings on time, etc. Indicators = WhatsApp response time, lead-triage accuracy.
- **TRM-aware management.** New pre-sales analyst → directive. Senior CoE operator → delegate by outcome.
- **Decision discipline.** L10 IDS sessions use Grove's free-discussion-then-clear-decision-then-full-support pattern. No re-litigating decisions next week.

### Meet's personal time audit

Grove's "managerial time as currency" — Meet should literally audit his weekly calendar:
- What % went to high-leverage activities (hiring, strategic decisions, content production, 1:1s with direct reports, public brand)?
- What % went to low-leverage activities (operational tasks Meet should have delegated, meetings that didn't need him, status updates)?
- Target: 70%+ high-leverage. If lower, restructure the week.

### StartupOS — Grove discipline pre-team

- Pre-team, much of HOM doesn't apply directly. But the *thinking mode* matters:
- Frame StartupOS as a production line: what's the throughput rate, where are the bottlenecks?
- The "Breakfast Factory" metaphor: even a 1-person operation is a production process. Map it.

### Cross-portfolio

- Every venture should have explicit Output metrics + Indicator metrics tracked weekly
- File at `30 Ventures/[Venture]/Scorecard.md`
- Refuse to discuss venture progress without these. Stories without numbers = anecdotes.

## Where the book is wrong (or limited)

- Grove writes from a 1980s Intel manufacturing context. Some examples are dated. The principles survive; some specifics (memo distribution, paper reports) don't.
- HOM assumes you have a team. For pre-team / solo founder phases (early StartupOS, Biggdate), the book has less to offer. Reach for [[Strategy/Working Backwards|Working Backwards]] or [[Strategy/The Hard Thing About Hard Things|Hard Things]] for that stage.
- Grove's "task-relevant maturity" model can be misapplied as "micromanage juniors". The principle is right, the application requires judgment.
- Light on the *people* side beyond 1:1s. Pair with [[Operating-Leadership/Radical Candor|Radical Candor]] for that layer.
