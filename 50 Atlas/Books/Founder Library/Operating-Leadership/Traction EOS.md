# Traction — Gino Wickman (EOS)

## Why it matters to Meet

The canonical book on the Entrepreneurial Operating System (EOS). Used by 100,000+ small + mid-market businesses worldwide. The framework's strength is its *simplicity* — the whole team can hold the model in their head. Meet is already running EOS at Finanshels CoE. This note is the canonical reference for the operating system underneath that.

## One-line thesis

Six components — Vision, People, Data, Issues, Process, Traction — turn a chaotic small business into a coherent operating machine. Implement all six, with discipline, and the business runs without depending on the founder's daily energy.

## Core ideas

(Full breakdown in [[Ops-Execution/EOS|EOS framework note]] — this note covers the *book's* thinking, not just the framework.)

1. **The Vision/Traction Organizer (V/TO).** A 2-page document that captures the company's vision: core values, core focus, 10-year target, marketing strategy, 3-year picture, 1-year plan, quarterly rocks, issues list. Shared by all. Refreshed quarterly + annually. Without a V/TO, "alignment" is theater.

2. **People — right people, right seats.** Two tests: (a) *People Analyzer* — does the person embody the company's core values (above-the-line vs below-the-line)? (b) *GWC* — does the person Get the role, Want the role, have the Capacity for the role? People who fail GWC are in the wrong seat. People who fail Values are wrong for the company.

3. **Data — the Scorecard.** 5–15 weekly numbers that predict business health. Leading indicators (predict the future) over lagging (describe the past). Reviewed every L10.

4. **Issues — the IDS process.** Identify the real issue (often different from the surface symptom). Discuss with the team. Solve — meaning decide, assign owner, set deadline. Don't discuss without solving.

5. **Process — documented + followed.** Codify the 6–10 core processes (how we sell, deliver, onboard, hire). Train everyone. Follow them. Deviations become issues.

6. **Traction — Rocks + L10 Pulse + Quarterly Pulsing.** Quarterly Rocks = 3–7 priorities per leader for the quarter. Weekly L10s = 90-minute structured meeting (Segue → Scorecard → Rocks → Headlines → To-dos → IDS → Conclude → Rating). Quarterly Pulsing = full V/TO refresh.

### Cross-portfolio EOS adoption

- Finanshels = full EOS (team large enough)
- StartupOS = EOS-lite when team grows past 5
- Biggdate = EOS-lite when launched + team grows
- Solo / pre-team ventures = use OKRs instead; adopt EOS when team scales

### Common Meet-context adaptations

- **V/TO core values:** should be 3–5, specific, observable, used in hiring + firing decisions. Avoid generic ("integrity, excellence, teamwork" = no values). Write Finanshels-specific values that distinguish.
- **Rocks vs OKRs:** Finanshels CoE uses Rocks (Wickman's term). Strategic initiatives like AI demand engine could use OKR overlay for tighter measurement. Both can coexist.
- **L10 ratings:** end every meeting with 1–10 rating from every attendee. Trending below 8 = meeting is broken. Trending above 9 = team is too comfortable, raise the bar.

## Where the book is wrong (or limited)

- Wickman writes for *small-to-mid traditional businesses* (10–250 people, often blue-collar or services). Some examples don't translate to tech/SaaS. Adapt liberally.
- The EOS Implementer industry (paid consultants) can over-systematize. EOS works best when the *team* owns it, not when an external consultant runs it weekly forever.
- Light on AI-native operating considerations — EOS is pre-AI. For ventures where AI agents are core to operations (StartupOS), supplement with [[Strategy/Working Backwards|Working Backwards]] and [[Operating-Leadership/High Output Management|HOM]].
- Some chapters feel like sales pitches for EOS-the-product. Skim those.
- The 90-minute L10 timing is dogmatic. For high-velocity tech teams, 60 min often works better. Adapt.
