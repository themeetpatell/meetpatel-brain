# Multipliers — Liz Wiseman

## Why it matters to Meet

Wiseman (former Oracle exec, now leadership researcher) studied managers across hundreds of organizations and found a sharp pattern: some leaders make everyone around them *smarter and more capable* (Multipliers). Others make everyone *dumber and less capable* (Diminishers). The same person, with two different managers, performs 2× differently. For Meet running a portfolio with multiple teams, becoming a Multiplier is the single highest-leverage personal investment. Diminishing behaviors are easy to fall into — especially as a founder who can do the work themselves.

## One-line thesis

The best leaders amplify the intelligence and capability of everyone around them — by asking hard questions, granting real ownership, creating intense challenges, and getting out of the way. The worst do the opposite, even unintentionally.

## Core ideas

1. **The 5 disciplines of Multipliers.**
   - **Talent Magnet** — attract and deploy top talent; not just hire, but find their genius and put it to use
   - **Liberator** — create an environment that demands the best from people while making it safe to fail
   - **Challenger** — define stretch challenges that go beyond what the team thinks possible
   - **Debate Maker** — drive sound decisions through rigorous, structured debate
   - **Investor** — give people full ownership of outcomes; teach, coach, then let them run

2. **The 5 corresponding Diminisher patterns:**
   - Empire Builder (hoards talent) → fix with Talent Magnet
   - Tyrant (climate of fear) → fix with Liberator
   - Know-It-All (gives answers) → fix with Challenger
   - Decision Maker (decides alone) → fix with Debate Maker
   - Micromanager (controls execution) → fix with Investor

3. **Accidental Diminishers.** Even well-intentioned, smart leaders unintentionally diminish their teams by being *too helpful*, *too knowledgeable*, or *too fast*. The "rescuer", the "idea fountain", the "always-on responder" — all good-hearted, all diminishing.

4. **The 2× multiplier effect.** Wiseman's data: people deliver up to 2× their normal output for Multiplier leaders. *Same person, same skill set.* The leverage of becoming a Multiplier is enormous.

5. **Ask hard questions, don't give answers.** Multipliers' signature move: in conversations, they ask questions that force the team to think — rather than handing down the answer they already have.

## Killer quotes

> *"Multipliers extract full intelligence from people. Diminishers underutilize people and leave capability on the table."*

> *"Genius is contextual. Find the genius in each person and put it to work."*

> *"The best leaders are not the ones with the answers. They're the ones who ask the questions that surface the answers from their team."*

> *"Even the most well-intentioned, smartest leaders accidentally diminish those around them — usually by being too helpful, too directive, or too quick."*

> *"A Multiplier's standard is high — but so is their belief that you can hit it."*

## How to apply

### Meet's diminishing risks (specific to founder context)

Founders who *can* do every job often *do* every job — that's accidental diminishing. Meet should audit himself for these patterns:

- **Idea fountain** — generating ideas faster than the team can execute. Slow down. Let them generate first.
- **Always-on responder** — replying instantly to every message. Trains the team to ask instead of decide. Pause before answering.
- **Rescuer** — jumping in to fix when a junior team member is struggling. Sit with the discomfort. Coach instead.
- **Default-decider** — being the one who decides because it's faster. Use RAPID. Push decisions down.

### Finanshels CoE — Multiplier mode

- **Talent Magnet:** When hiring CoE roles, ask not "can they do the job?" but "what's their genius?" Hire so each person has at least one area they're *uniquely good at* — then deploy them there.
- **Challenger:** Set CoE goals that genuinely stretch. "Hit a 30-minute average response time" feels impossible. Multiplier sets it anyway and asks "what would it take?" Diminisher sets achievable goals so they look like a hero when hit.
- **Investor:** When the CoE team owns a problem, Meet's job is to teach and step back — not to insert himself when execution gets bumpy.
- **Debate Maker:** L10 IDS sessions should have *real debate*, not consensus theater. Encourage dissent before decision. Lock decision afterward.

### StartupOS — Multiplier mode for the founding team

- Pre-team, much of this doesn't apply directly. But the cofounder relationships and early-hire dynamics are *foundational*. Get Multiplier patterns right from hire #1.

### Personal — Multiplier discipline check

- Weekly review question: *"This week, did I make my team smarter or did I make them dependent?"*
- If the answer is *dependent* — restructure the next week. Push at least one decision back to its rightful owner. Ask three more questions than you answer.

## Where the book is wrong (or limited)

- Wiseman's research is correlational — she studied leaders perceived as Multipliers vs Diminishers. Causation is harder to nail down. The patterns are still useful even if the 2× number is fuzzy.
- The 5 disciplines occasionally overlap. Liberator + Challenger especially. Don't get hung up on which discipline is being applied — focus on the underlying behaviors.
- Less actionable on *what to do when you've been diminishing for a while*. Recovery is harder than the book implies.
- Some chapters lean into corporate-leadership-coach vocabulary. Skim those.
