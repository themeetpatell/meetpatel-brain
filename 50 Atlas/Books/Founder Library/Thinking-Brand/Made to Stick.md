# Made to Stick — Chip Heath & Dan Heath

## Why it matters to Meet

The definitive book on *why some ideas survive and others die*. The Heath brothers analyzed urban legends, news stories, ad campaigns, and academic research to extract the principles. Their finding: sticky ideas share six properties (SUCCESs framework). For Meet — building a founder brand, sharpening venture pitches, writing positioning, drafting LinkedIn posts — this is the canonical communication discipline. Every idea Meet puts in public should pass the SUCCESs check.

## One-line thesis

Sticky ideas are Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, and Story-shaped (SUCCESs). The Heath's didn't invent these qualities — they identified what makes ideas naturally compelling and showed how to design for it.

## Core ideas — the SUCCESs framework

1. **Simple.** Find the core idea. Strip away the rest. Not "dumb down" — *prioritize* and *compact*. The commander's intent: one sentence that captures the whole.

2. **Unexpected.** Surprise opens the gate. Then *interest* keeps it open. Break a pattern, violate an expectation — the brain pays attention.

3. **Concrete.** Sensory details. Specific examples. Numbers and verbs over abstractions. "Lower-cost flights" vs "the airline you can afford to fly to your grandmother's funeral".

4. **Credible.** Borrow credibility (authority, statistics, testable claims). Anti-authority can work too (former smoker on smoking, ex-cop on policing). The "Sinatra test" — if you can name one prominent example, the audience will extrapolate.

5. **Emotional.** Specific human stakes. People care about people, not statistics. "1 dying child" generates more donation than "5,000 starving children" — the *identifiable victim effect*.

6. **Story.** Stories are simulation + inspiration. They show how the principle plays out in real action — and they're memorable because narrative is how human memory works.

7. **The Curse of Knowledge.** The biggest enemy of sticky ideas: experts who can't remember not-knowing what they now know. They explain in jargon, skip the foundation, and lose the audience. Cure: brutal awareness of the audience's starting point.

## Killer quotes

> *"Once we know something, we find it hard to imagine what it was like not to know it. Our knowledge has 'cursed' us."*

> *"It's hard to get a Made-to-Stick idea wrong. It's almost as hard to get it right by accident."*

> *"To find the core, we've got to ruthlessly prioritize. The Commander's Intent is the simplest articulation of the most important thing."*

> *"If you say three things, you don't say anything."*

> *"To strip an idea down to its core, we must master excluding superfluous and tangential elements. But that's the easy part. The hard part is the second piece: weeding out the *important* but not the most important. Choose the *most* important."*

## How to apply

### Meet's brand voice — SUCCESs audit for every public piece

Every LinkedIn post, every newsletter section, every essay should be checked:

- **Simple:** what's the one thing the reader will remember? If you can't say it in 8 words, rewrite.
- **Unexpected:** what's the angle the reader didn't predict? If they did, it's filler.
- **Concrete:** are there specific names, numbers, places, moments? Or abstractions only?
- **Credible:** does the claim borrow real credibility? Or is it founder-vibes?
- **Emotional:** does the piece make the reader feel something — recognition, frustration, hope, conviction?
- **Story:** is there narrative shape? Or is it bullet-list theater?

### Venture positioning — SUCCESs audit

For each venture, write the one-sentence positioning, then check:

**Finanshels current sentence:** *"AI-native accounting platform for UAE SMBs."*

- Simple ✓ (clear)
- Unexpected ✗ (every fintech says this)
- Concrete ✗ (no specific numbers, no specific outcomes)
- Credible ✗ (no proof embedded)
- Emotional ✗ (no human stake)
- Story ✗ (no narrative)

**Re-write attempt:** *"Finanshels handles your VAT, AML, and books for UAE SMBs — so you can run your business instead of worrying about the regulator."*
- Simple ✓
- Unexpected: marginally improved (mentions the *fear* — regulator)
- Concrete ✓ (specific filings)
- Credible: needs proof point embedded (X,000 SMBs served)
- Emotional ✓ (worry, regulator, running your business — human stake)
- Story: implied (you, the owner, vs the regulator)

Better. Iterate further.

### StartupOS — sticky-ideas check for the wedge narrative

- Whichever StartupOS wedge gets chosen, the *story of why* must pass SUCCESs
- Test: can a 5-year-old founder repeat the wedge after hearing it once?
- If no — too complicated. Simplify or kill.

### Curse of Knowledge — Meet-specific risk

- Meet operates with deep context across 8 ventures + UAE/GCC + AI-native + regulated industry + diaspora demographics + founder-operator role
- When writing publicly, this *cursed knowledge* makes him skip foundation the reader needs
- Discipline: before posting, imagine a smart 25-year-old founder with no UAE context reading. Can they follow? If no — add the foundation.

## Where the book is wrong (or limited)

- The Heaths write from a marketing/copy frame. Some examples are dated (early 2000s urban legends). The framework still applies.
- "Sticky" isn't always the goal. Some ideas need to be precise (legal contracts, regulatory filings, technical docs) and should *not* be optimized for sticky-ness.
- The framework can be checklist-applied superficially. Real stickiness requires craft, not just framework-adherence.
- Light on *long-form* — the SUCCESs frame is best for slogans, ads, posts. For essays + books, sticky principles apply but the architecture is different.
