# Content Writing Skill — The Meet Patel Writing System

This document defines how I write, how I should sound, what to avoid, and how to produce content that feels fully aligned with my taste, standards, and identity.

It is a working writing skill file for me, my team, and any AI system writing on my behalf.

---

# 1. Core Writing Objective

My writing should feel like it came from a founder-operator with taste, pressure, clarity, and emotional intelligence.

It should sound like someone who:
- has built things
- has carried responsibility
- understands business deeply
- sees people clearly
- values words
- hates fluff
- moves fast
- still has soul

The output should never feel templated, over-optimized, MBA-ish, startup-bro-ish, or machine-made.

The writing should create one or more of these effects:
- clarity
- authority
- intrigue
- emotional truth
- momentum
- trust
- sharpness
- memorability

Every piece of writing should pass this test:

## It should be
- clear
- useful
- distinct
- well-paced
- non-generic
- readable out loud
- free of filler
- relevant to the audience
- strong in first impression

- fake-deep

- corporate

---

# 4. Golden Rules

## Rule 1: Say something real
Do not produce empty motivation, recycled startup advice, or content that sounds like it exists only to fill a calendar.

## Rule 2: Every sentence should earn its place
If a sentence adds no clarity, tension, flavor, or movement, cut it.

## Rule 3: Strong first line matters
The opening should pull attention naturally.
Not with clickbait.
With truth, surprise, sharp framing, or emotional recognition.

## Rule 4: Sound human
Use natural language.
Avoid stiff phrasing.
Avoid sounding like a corporate deck or LinkedIn content machine.

## Rule 5: Specific beats vague
Use concrete observations, strong framing, and precise wording.

## Rule 6: Respect rhythm
Some ideas need short lines.
Some need flow.
Good writing has movement.

## Rule 7: Write with taste
Do not overdo punchiness.
Do not overdo slang.
Do not overdo drama.
Everything should feel controlled.

## Rule 8: Keep the dignity of the reader
Do not talk down.
Do not oversimplify.
Do not manipulate cheaply.

---

# 5. Hard Bans

These patterns should be avoided unless I explicitly ask for them.

## Banned structure patterns
- “not X, but Y”
- “you’re not this, you’re that”
- contrast-duel writing
- parallel-truth lines that feel like Instagram therapy content
- overused guru formatting
- fake revelation phrasing
- too many one-line sentences in a row with no depth

## Banned tone patterns
- corporate inspiration
- startup-bro chest-thumping
- hollow thought leadership
- fake vulnerability
- preachy life lessons
- overuse of rhetorical questions
- obvious AI phrasing

## Banned vocabulary style
Avoid overusing words like:
- revolutionize
- unlock
- seamless
- game-changing
- leverage as filler
- robust
- cutting-edge
- next-gen
- disruption when lazy
- supercharge
- empower when vague

Use them only if the sentence truly earns it.

# 7. Writing by Content Type

## A. LinkedIn posts
LinkedIn posts should feel sharp, alive, and readable.

### What they should do
- hook fast
- say one real thing well
- create reaction or recognition
- carry authority without sounding inflated
- feel written by a real founder/operator

### Style
- flowing prose preferred
- minimal formatting
- no gimmicky boxes
- no fake poem style unless intentionally artistic
- no over-bulleted structure unless the post is tactical

### Ideal LinkedIn ingredients
- a strong opening line
- a real observation
- a useful insight or angle
- optional lived texture
- a satisfying close

### Good LinkedIn closing styles
- decisive takeaway
- sharp final line
- subtle invitation to think
- quiet power

### Avoid on LinkedIn
- generic hustle language
- cliché founder lessons
- obvious engagement bait
- fake storytelling arcs
- preachy “here are 5 lessons” unless genuinely useful

---

# 9. Hook Styles That Fit Me

Hooks should feel intelligent and sharp.

## Good hook types
- a truth people feel but rarely say
- a sharp observation
- a clean contradiction in reality, without using the banned contrast formula
- a sentence that reframes the situation immediately
- a line with pressure, consequence, or emotional signal

## Examples of the feel
- Every founder says they want scale. Very few build for it.
- Most teams are drowning in motion and calling it progress.
- The real cost of bad positioning is not confusion. It is delay.
- Some products fail in the market. Others fail in the first sentence used to explain them.

These are examples of energy, not templates to copy lazily.

# 11. Audience Sensitivity

Different audiences need different energy.

## Founders / operators
More direct. More strategic. Higher signal. Faster pacing.

## Customers
Clearer. Warmer. More outcome-led. Less internal language.

## Investors
Sharper logic. Stronger framing. Bigger inevitability. Cleaner market language.

## Teams
Simple. respectful. operational. motivating without sounding fake.

## Personal brand audience
Mix intelligence with humanity. Let the person show, not just the framework.

- understanding what people feel

## Remove anything that feels like
- default LLM rhythm

- generic content-creator hooks
- formulaic social media structure
- synthetic inspiration

## Add back
- stronger opinion

# 14. My Writing Workflow

Use this sequence when drafting on my behalf.

## Step 1: Define the job
What is this piece trying to do?
- persuade
- explain
- position
- sell
- attract
- provoke thought
- create trust
- drive action

## Step 2: Define the audience
Who is reading this?
What do they care about?
What do they already know?
What are they tired of hearing?

## Step 3: Find the sharp angle
What is the one real thing worth saying here?

## Step 4: Draft with energy
Write fast enough to keep life in it.

## Step 5: Cut the fake parts
Remove filler, generic language, and anything that sounds machine-made.

## Step 6: Tune rhythm
Read it out loud mentally.
Make sure it moves.

## Step 7: Strengthen the first and last line
That is where memory is made.

---

# 15. Editing Checklist

Before approving any draft, ask:

- Is the first line strong?
- Is this saying something real?
- Does it sound like me?
- Is there any filler?
- Is any sentence too generic?
- Did we accidentally use a banned contrast format?
- Is the rhythm good?
- Is the tone right for the audience?
- Would a smart reader feel this was written by a real person with taste?
- Is the final line worth reaching?
