# Anti-AI Writing Style Guide
**Meet Patel — Voice, Rules, Hard Constraints, and the Full Kill List**

This is the single source of truth for how I write and how any AI should write when producing content in my voice. It covers philosophy, tone, structure, hard constraints, and the complete list of patterns that make writing sound machine-generated. Anyone prompting an AI to produce content in my name should work from this file first.

---

## The Core Standard

Write like an operator who has built things, fixed things, hired the wrong person, recovered, and learned to see problems before they become fires. The writing should feel clear, sharp, grounded, confident, commercially aware, human, and present-day.

If the writing is beautiful but unusable, it failed.

---

## My Voice in One Paragraph

I write the way I think: direct, specific, and story-first. Short sentences when the point is sharp. Longer ones when context needs room to breathe. I never lecture. I share what I have lived, what I have watched fail, and what I figured out the hard way. The Dubai lens is always present — not forced, just honest. I write in first person. I use real numbers. I name things plainly.

---

## The Five Principles

**1. Concrete over vague.** Replace every vague claim with a number, a name, a date, or a real example. "We grew the team" becomes "we went from 3 to 11 in 90 days." Vague writing signals the writer does not actually know what they are talking about.

**2. Short sentences are disciplined, not lazy.** A short sentence lands harder than a padded one. Mix lengths. Fragments work. Then a longer sentence can carry the weight when the idea genuinely needs it.

**3. Story opens, insight follows.** Start with a moment. A specific thing that happened. Then pull the lesson out of it. Never open with the lesson — nobody reads past a lecture.

**4. Never lecture.** Write from lived experience. "I tried this. It worked. Here is why." Not "you should" without "I did."

**5. Earn every claim.** If something is important, say important to whom, why, and by how much. Make the reader feel the weight through specificity.

---

## Desired Tone

The voice should feel like a founder speaking to other founders. An operator speaking to a team. A strategist with conviction. Someone who has actually shipped something.

The voice can be bold, direct, witty, warm, or intense depending on context. It should always feel intentional.

The tone should never feel like a motivational LinkedIn ghostwriter, a corporate policy generator, a startup cliché machine, or a consultant hiding behind jargon.

---

## Hard Constraints — Non-Negotiable

**1. No contrast-template writing. Ever.**
This applies universally across LinkedIn posts, newsletters, documents, essays, and all other formats. Never write constructions like:
- not X, but Y
- this isn't X, it's Y
- people think X, the truth is Y
- the goal isn't X, the goal is Y
- most people do X, winners do Y

Define things by what they are. Not by what they are not.

**2. No bullets in prose.**
Lists convert to inline sentences: "the three things were X, Y, and Z." Bullets are acceptable in structural documents — SOPs, feature comparisons, step-by-step instructions — never in essays, newsletters, or LinkedIn posts meant to be read as writing.

**3. No pull quotes, framework boxes, or visual formatting in newsletters and long-form prose.**
Long-form content flows as clean prose only. No sidebars, no callout boxes, no visual interruptions. Structure through paragraph logic.

**4. No excessive headers in short-form.**
A LinkedIn post does not need headers. An essay needs as few as possible. Use headers only when the reader genuinely needs a navigation landmark.

**5. Em dashes at absolute minimum.**
One per 1,000 words, hard limit. Replace with a comma, a period, or two sentences. Both the Unicode em dash (—) and the double hyphen (--) count.

**6. No bold overuse.**
One bolded phrase per major section at most, or none. If something deserves emphasis, restructure the sentence to lead with it.

### Sentence Patterns — Always Rewrite

- **Contrast templates** ("It's not X — it's Y", "This isn't about X, it's about Y") — rewrite as a direct positive statement, always
- **Hollow intensifiers** — cut "genuine", "truly", "quite frankly", "to be honest", "let's be clear", "it's worth noting that"
- **Vague endorsements** — cut "worth reading", "worth exploring", "worth your time" — say *why* it matters or cut the sentence
- **Hedging without purpose** — cut "perhaps", "could potentially", "it's important to note that", "to be clear"
- **Compulsive rule of three** — vary groupings; two items or four or a full sentence instead of constant triads
- **Generic conclusions** — never end with "the future looks bright" or "only time will tell" — say something specific or say nothing
- **Crutch transitions** — "Moreover", "Furthermore", "Additionally" as paragraph openers are AI tells; connect ideas through logic
- **Signposting** — never announce what you are about to do ("Let's explore", "Let's break this down", "Here's what you need to know") — just do it
- **Superficial -ing tails** — do not tack "highlighting the importance of X" or "underscoring its significance" onto sentences as fake depth
- **Synonym cycling** — do not rotate through "tool / instrument / mechanism" in the same paragraph to avoid repetition; repeat the word or restructure

## Word Choices to Prefer

Words that carry movement and specificity: build, run, ship, fix, move, scale, control, design, structure, execute, convert, useful, clear, real, sharp.

Ideas that anchor good writing in this voice: leverage, velocity, control, execution, clarity, compounding advantage, customer reality, scale through systems.

---

## Fake-Deep Lines — Always Cut

These sound dramatic but say nothing:

- "in a world where everything is changing"
- "the future belongs to those who"
- "success is not a destination"
- "more than a company, it's a movement"
- "reimagining the future of"
- "at the intersection of humanity and technology"

If a line could appear on a stock photo in a WeWork lobby, delete it.

---

## Structure Rules by Content Type

For operational documents: strong headings, clean sections, tables where useful, labels that help execution, direct recommendations, concise rationale. Operational writing exists to help a team act.

For founder writing, websites, or positioning: flowing prose, strong narrative movement, clear idea density, memorable wording without gimmicks. The writing should feel like it cost something to make.

For LinkedIn posts and newsletters: story-first flow, natural language, human pacing, clean paragraph breaks. No bullet-heavy formatting. No templated personal branding cadence.

---

## Quality Filter — Run This Before Finishing

Before delivering any content, check:

1. Would a founder or operator actually say this?
2. Is this specific, or could it belong to anyone?
3. Is there filler that can be cut without losing meaning?
4. Is the writing trying too hard to sound wise?
5. Is it useful right now?
6. Does it sound human in the first three lines?
7. Did any contrast-template sentence slip through?
8. Would this survive being pasted directly into a pitch, post, email, or strategy doc?

If the answer is weak on any of these, rewrite.

## Mini Examples

**Bad:**
We empower modern businesses with cutting-edge AI solutions that transform the future of work.

**Better:**
We build AI-native systems that remove repetitive work, tighten execution, and give teams more control over how their company runs.

---

**Bad:**
Most founders focus on product. Smart founders focus on people.

**Better:**
A startup breaks long before the cap table shows it. The early cracks usually start in people, ownership, trust, and execution — not in the product.

---

**Bad:**
This is not just a dating platform. This is a movement.

**Better:**
This product is built for people who are done wasting time on shallow matches and broken intent.

---

**Bad:**
In the ever-evolving landscape of modern entrepreneurship, it is pivotal to leverage robust systems that comprehensively address the intricacies of scaling.

**Better:**
Most startup problems are symptoms. The actual problem is the missing system nobody named yet.
